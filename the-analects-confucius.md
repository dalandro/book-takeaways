## My framing towards the software industry: Balance between adhering to rites, and updating/rejecting them

Here's what I took away from recently reading The Analects; 

### On following "rites" (practices, rituals, customs, etc in a society) 
- On one hand, he considers following rites as paramount because they encode the accumulated wisdom of times before. 
- On another hand, he stresses that rites must be questioned deeply, as the customs that served a purpose and principle that applied before may no longer be relevant or effective in your current time. 

### On the dualism of Learning vs Thinking
- Learning allows you to attain already-discovered knowledge - you should take advantage of the effort of those who have come before you.
- Thinking allows you to weigh the merits or correctness of knowledge and discover new ideas. 

### On principles, and their relation to rites/practices 
- It is both moral and useful to understand principles behind rites. 
- Rites can be determined or synthesized based on principles. Conversely, principles can be decoded from rites (side note: Rites could have been shaped by principles, but principles in a society can be shaped by rites, and you can use this to compare societies with diverging sets of principles)
- Where appropriate, principles allow you to reject or modify antiquated rites. Maybe society has changed over time, such that they no longer apply. Maybe they are best applied considering the nuances of different situations. The rejection is supposedly because of a deep understanding you have for their original and/or intended purpose.
- If a rite is worth following, based on your understanding of the principles behind it, you should do so with reverence for it and the principles it draws meaning from. This supposedly allows you to be as effective as needed in achieving the purpose it serves. 

These ideas apply to software engineering. For example, ideas like CI/CD and the DevOps movement have principles that result in practices. Outside of software engineering, KPIs have a similar story - what may have been useful for measurement at one point in time may no longer serve its purpose as time goes by
A conclusion we could draw is that the practices that you apply should depend on circumstances - there must be principles behind them, and to each situation you could apply a unique consideration of different principles to derive a suitable approach.

## Misc points, in no particular order
- One of the author's interpretation of his style was to avoid prescribing a framework or methodology of thought, but instead the analects showcases a thought process, almost like it shows Confucius thinking aloud, interpreted to show how he would think deeply on a case-by-case basis.
  - After reading Chin's introduction, I realise I have seen the "thinking aloud" characteristic in a tv show from a character based on the ancient statesman and legalist philosopher Shang Yang. Maybe that was intentional - he was set up as an admirable character, so maybe they are trying to show that he embodied fundamental aspects of Confucianism. 
- Related to the above point, there is an emphasis on strongly weighing both ends of a problem before finalising an answer.
